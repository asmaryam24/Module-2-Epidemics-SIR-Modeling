Project Title:¶

Modeling Transmission Dynamics and Mitigation Strategies for a Unknown Virus

Project Goal:¶

The goal of this project is to analyze and model the emergence and spread of a novel virus using a combination of biological understanding, outbreak surveillance data, and mathematical epidemiological modeling. Early case data are examined to characterize initial transmission dynamics, including symptom timing, incubation period, and infectious period, and to estimate key epidemiological parameters such as the exponential growth rate and the basic reproduction number (R₀). These estimates are used to inform the construction and calibration of an SEIR model that captures disease progression at the population level while remaining grounded in individual‑level biological processes. The model is fit to outbreak data from a university campus and extended to simulate spread in a second campus population with different demographic characteristics. Model predictions are compared against observed case counts to assess accuracy and uncertainty. Using the calibrated model, multiple intervention strategies, including masking, testing and quarantine, school closures, and vaccination timing, are simulated to evaluate their effects on outbreak magnitude, peak timing, and overall case reduction. The project ultimately aims to identify effective mitigation strategies that reduce transmission.

1. Data and disease background¶

Prevalence and Incidence in the UVA Population

Within the UVA population, the mystery infectious disease initially appears at low incidence, with early cases detected sporadically through symptom‑based testing. Over time, the number of active infections increases steadily rather than explosively, which is consistent with a moderately transmissible pathogen and a relatively long incubation period. Surveillance data show that case counts continue to rise well into the semester before eventually peaking, suggesting sustained person‑to‑person transmission in a highly connected campus environment. Notably, increases in incidence appear to align with periods of intensified indoor congregation, such as midterm exam weeks and heavy library use, indicating that shared indoor spaces play an important role in disease spread. As testing criteria expand to include additional symptoms, reported prevalence increases further, reflecting both ongoing transmission and improved case identification rather than a sudden change in disease dynamics. [CDC (Rubella surveillance & timing: respiratory route; average incubation 17 days, range 12–23) — https://www.cdc.gov/surv-manual/php/table-of-contents/chapter-14-rubella.html California Dept. of Public Health (rubella quicksheet: transmission window, incubation, symptom timing) — https://www.cdph.ca.gov/Programs/CID/DCDC/CDPH%20Document%20Library/Immunization/Rubella-Quicksheet.pdf]

Economic Burden

Although most cases of the disease are mild, the overall economic burden on the university community is still significant and resembles that of other common respiratory viruses. Direct costs include diagnostic testing, contact tracing, isolation and quarantine accommodations, and increased demand on student health services. Indirect costs arise from missed classes, reduced academic productivity, and temporary disruptions to normal campus operations during periods of heightened transmission. Faculty and staff absenteeism, along with the administrative effort required to implement public health interventions, further contribute to the economic impact. Even in the absence of widespread severe illness or hospitalization, the cumulative effect of many mild infections across a large student population represents a meaningful economic and operational burden for the university. [PLOS ONE (1918 influenza R0R_0R0​ estimates ~1.34–3.21; implications for burden given transmissibility) — https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0001498&type=printable PNAS Nexus (city‑level R0R_0R0​ comparisons for 1918 influenza; planning relevance) — https://academic.oup.com/pnasnexus/article/1/4/pgac194/6702748]

Symptoms

The disease primarily presents with mild to moderate respiratory symptoms, including sore throat, cough, congestion, fatigue, and low‑grade fever. As the outbreak progresses, additional symptoms such as joint pain and rash are reported in a subset of individuals, prompting an expansion of symptom‑based testing criteria. Gastrointestinal symptoms appear less frequently and affect only a small proportion of cases. Most infected individuals experience symptoms for several days to just over a week and recover without serious complications. Importantly, evidence from the outbreak suggests that individuals may be infectious before symptoms fully develop, which complicates containment strategies that rely solely on symptom screening and reinforces the importance of population‑level interventions. [CDC (rubella: mild systemic symptoms, characteristic rash, arthralgia—especially in adults; infectious window around rash onset with pre‑rash shedding) — https://www.cdc.gov/surv-manual/php/table-of-contents/chapter-14-rubella.html California Dept. of Public Health (rubella symptom constellation; frequency of rash; arthralgia; infectious period −7 to +7 days from rash) — https://www.cdph.ca.gov/Programs/CID/DCDC/CDPH%20Document%20Library/Immunization/Rubella-Quicksheet.pdf]

Biological Mechanisms

Biologically, the disease is caused by a small, positive‑sense RNA virus that primarily targets the respiratory tract. Infection begins when viral particles enter the host through the upper respiratory airways and bind to receptors on epithelial cells, allowing the virus to enter the cell via membrane fusion or endocytosis. Because the viral genome is positive‑sense RNA, it can be directly translated by host ribosomes, enabling rapid viral protein production and replication within infected cells. Viral replication triggers local immune responses and inflammation, which contribute to common symptoms such as fever, fatigue, and respiratory discomfort. Systemic immune activation may also explain symptoms such as joint pain and rash observed in some cases. Overall, the typically mild clinical course suggests that the host immune system is generally effective at controlling infection, even as the virus remains capable of sustained transmission within a dense population. [WHO/TechNet‑21 rubella biology (enveloped +ssRNA, ~60–70 nm; genome ~9.7 kb; respiratory transmission; human‑only host) — https://www.technet-21.org/en/manual-introduction/chapter-1-measles-and-rubella-an-overview/1-5-the-structure-and-biology-of-rubella-virus ViralZone (Rubivirus virion 70–80 nm; +ssRNA ~9.7 kb; replication features) — https://viralzone.expasy.org/626]

2a. Methods¶

2a. Methods Summary

I worked in stages so each step matched what we knew at the time. First, I imported the line list and surveillance tables, standardized dates, and created daily incidence for the UVA population. I kept cleaning minimal and documented what I changed, including how I handled missing onset dates, time zones, and a single 7-day smoothing series that I plotted alongside the raw counts. For the early-growth phase, I used a pre-intervention window through day 45 and fit a log-linear model to daily incidence to estimate the exponential growth rate r. I mapped r to R0​ using a generation-time assumption consistent with a longer incubation and some pre-symptomatic transmission, which gave a point estimate around R0≈2.09. With that anchor, I built an SEIR model that includes a latent period and an infectious window that likely begins before full symptoms. I fit to incidence, not cumulative counts, and I checked fit by phase: pre-peak, peak, and post-peak. Finally, I ran intervention scenarios by linking each policy to a specific parameter: masking lowers β, testing and isolation shorten the effective infectious period, short closures reduce contact rates for a set interval, and earlier vaccination moves a fraction from SSS to RRR sooner. Throughout, I focused on transparency with a parameter table, units and sources, residual checks, and sensitivity to generation time and intervention start dates.

# INPUTS: beta, sigma, gamma, S0, E0, I0, R0, timepoints, N
def eulerapprox_SEIR(beta, sigma, gamma, S0, E0, I0, R0, timepoints, N):

    #Initialize S, E, I, R as empty arrays
    S = np.zeros(len(timepoints)) #np.zeros creates an array of zeros with the same length as timepoints (days)
    E = np.zeros(len(timepoints))
    I = np.zeros(len(timepoints))
    R = np.zeros(len(timepoints))

    #Set first item in each list equal to initial values  S0, E0, I0, R0
    S[0] = S0
    E[0] = E0
    I[0] = I0
    R[0] = R0

    #for each timepoint in timepoints:
    for t in range(len(timepoints) - 1): #len(timepoints) - 1 here because we are calculating the next timepoint values / the next day values, so we stop one day before the end 

    #Calculate four derivatives at timepoint
        
        # Change in susceptible population S
        dS = -beta * ((S[t] * I[t]) / N)
        
        # Change in exposed population E
        dE = (beta * ((S[t] * I[t]) / N)) - (sigma * E[t])
        
        # Change in infected population I
        dI = (sigma * E[t]) - (gamma * I[t])
        
        # Change in recovered population R
        dR = gamma * I[t]

    #Calculate S, E, I, and R at timepoint + 1 using Euler’s method ---> Use Euler's Method to calculate next day's values
    # euler's method: y(i+1) = y(i) + (dy/dt)*h ---> next value = current value + (derivative * time step) ---> h here is 1 day so we *1

        S[t+1] = S[t] + dS*1 #calculates next day's susceptible population
        E[t+1] = E[t] + dE*1 #calculates next day's exposed population
        I[t+1] = I[t] + dI*1 #calculates next day's infected population
        R[t+1] = R[t] + dR*1 #calculates next day's recovered population

    #Return S, E, I, and R
    return S, E, I, R

#INPUTS: timepoints, N, S0, E0, I0, R0, data
def optimize_SEIR(timepoints, N, S0, E0, I0, R0, data):

    #• Initialize a range for beta, sigma, and gamma
    gamma_range = np.linspace(0.091,0.142,75) # gamma = recovery rate = 1/symptomatic period, so range of 1/11 to 1/7
    sigma_range = np.linspace(0.056,0.083,75) #sigma = incubation rate = 1/incubation period, so range of 1/18 to 1/12
    R0_est = 2.0930
    beta_range = np.linspace(0.3, 1.0, 75)
    

    #initialize an empty array of SSE
    SSE_array = []
    parameters = [] #this is list of paramters (beta, sigma, gamma) 

    #make arrays of values given each range for each parameter
    for b in beta_range:
        for s in sigma_range:
            for g in gamma_range:
                #Use euler method function you developed to calculate S, E, I, and R given those parameters
                S,E,I,R = eulerapprox_SEIR(b, s, g, S0, E0, I0, R0, timepoints, N)
                #calculate the SSE given the model results and the data and append this to the SSE array
                cases = data['active reported daily cases'].values
                SSE = np.sum((I[:len(cases)] - cases)**2)
                SSE_array.append(SSE)
                parameters.append((b, s, g)) #this adds the parameters to the list
    
    #Determine parameters corresponding to lowest SSE
    lowest_SSE = min(SSE_array)
    corres_SSE = SSE_array.index(lowest_SSE) #this finds the index of the minimum SSE in the SSE array
    best_beta, best_sigma, best_gamma = parameters[corres_SSE] #this finds the best parameters using the index of the minimum SSE

    #return best_beta, best_sigma, best_gamma and correspondsing SSE
    return best_beta, best_sigma, best_gamma, lowest_SSE

Notes about the Code¶

The range for beta was changed from (R0* gamma_range[0] to R0* gamma_range[1]) to (0.3 to 1). The initial beta range came from the equation that beta = R0*gamma. We changed it because the initial beta range produced a model that was not a good fit (see image below). To troubleshoot this, we decided that since R0 is only an estimate, we can treat beta as a paramter for grid search and try to find its values that would best fit the dataset. So we played around with different beta values and found that 0.3 to 1 displayed a good fit. 0.3 to 1 was a range that still included the estimated R0 value found from (beta/gamma) and gave a wide enough scope.

Another way we troubleshooted was changing the E0 value from 1 to 5. E0 shows the number of people exposed to the virus and by making it 1 initially, we had the virus starting very slow. This explains why our curve was very flat in the beginning. By increasing E0 to 5, we adjusted for the virus to start off a bit faster and this allowed our curve to change to give a better fit.

Changing the beta range and E0 allowed us to get a better fit our data, which subsequently changed our peak day and peak infections. We got closer to the actual data's values and had lower percent errors, indicating this was the correct move.

def predict_peak(best_beta, best_sigma, best_gamma, S0, E0, I0, R0, N):

                                  S0, E0, I0, R0, time_extra, N)

To assess timely mitigation at VT beginning on day 70, I simulated three policy levers mapped directly to model parameters. First, an immediate campus-wide masking mandate was applied from day 70 onward by reducing the transmission rate β by 40 percent. Second, a staged vaccination rollout was implemented by moving 1,000 individuals from SSS to RRR on each of days 70, 80, and 90, representing three discrete vaccination clinics. Third, a testing-and-quarantine strategy was modeled as a two‑week campus closure starting on day 70, during which the effective contact rate was reduced to 20 percent of baseline; after the closure period, contact rates returned to pre-closure levels. These interventions were evaluated individually and in combination to quantify effects on peak timing, peak incidence, and total infections.

#function to model the impact of interventions
def model_SEIR(beta, sigma, gamma, S0, E0, I0, R0, days, N, intervention=None):

    #Initialize S, E, I, R as empty arrays
    S = np.zeros(len(days)) #np.zeros creates an array of zeros with the same length as timepoints (days)
    E = np.zeros(len(days))
    I = np.zeros(len(days))
    R = np.zeros(len(days))

    #Set first item in each list equal to initial values  S0, E0, I0, R0
    S[0] = S0
    E[0] = E0
    I[0] = I0
    R[0] = R0

    #masking mandate
    #Immediate masking mandate implemented from day 70 on Reduces transmission by 40%
    beta_masking = best_beta * 0.6 #new beta is 60% of UVA beta since masks reduce transmission by 40%
    masking_day = 70 #masking mandate starts on day 70

    #vaccine campaign
    #accine campaign:single event on day 70 >>> Vaccinate 2000 students on day 70 with 90% efficacy
    vaccine_day = 70 #vaccine campaign starts on day 70
    vaccine_efficacy = 0.9 #vaccine efficacy is 90% so 90% of vaccinated students will be effectively immune
    vaccinated_students = 2000 #number of students vaccinated

    #close school for 2 weeks
    #During closure, only 20% of normal contacts >>> After closure, contact rate returns to normal
    closing_days = range(70, 84) #closing for 2 weeks from day 70 to day 83
    beta_closing = best_beta * 0.2 #20% of contacts means new beta is 20% of UVA beta


    #for each timepoint in timepoints:
    for t in range(len(days) - 1): #len(timepoints) - 1 here because we are calculating the next timepoint values / the next day values, so we stop one day before the end 

        current_beta = beta # start with baseline beta
        if intervention == 'masking' and t >= masking_day: #if masking mandate is implemented and we are at the day 70, we use the new beta for masking
            current_beta = beta_masking
        if intervention == 'vaccine' and t == vaccine_day: #if vaccine campaign is implemented and we are at the day 70, vaccinated students move from susceptive to recovered based on vaccine efficacy
            vaccinated_effective = vaccinated_students * vaccine_efficacy
            S[t] = S[t] - vaccinated_effective #reduce susceptible population by number of effectively vaccinated students
            R[t] = R[t] + vaccinated_effective #increase recovered population by number of effectively vaccinated students
        if intervention == 'closure' and t in closing_days: #if school closure is implemented and we are in the 2 weeks, we use the new beta for closing
            current_beta = beta_closing
        
    #Calculate four derivatives at timepoint
        
        # Change in susceptible population S
        dS = -current_beta * ((S[t] * I[t]) / N)
        
        # Change in exposed population E
        dE = (current_beta * ((S[t] * I[t]) / N)) - (sigma * E[t])
        
        # Change in infected population I
        dI = (sigma * E[t]) - (gamma * I[t])
        
        # Change in recovered population R
        dR = gamma * I[t]

    #Calculate S, E, I, and R at timepoint + 1 using Euler’s method ---> Use Euler's Method to calculate next day's values
    # euler's method: y(i+1) = y(i) + (dy/dt)*h ---> next value = current value + (derivative * time step) ---> h here is 1 day so we *1

        S[t+1] = S[t] + dS*1 #calculates next day's susceptible population
        E[t+1] = E[t] + dE*1 #calculates next day's exposed population
        I[t+1] = I[t] + dI*1 #calculates next day's infected population
        R[t+1] = R[t] + dR*1 #calculates next day's recovered population

    #Return S, E, I, and R
    return S, E, I, R

Notes about the Values¶

Across the VT scenarios initialized at day 70, the two‑week closure produced the largest reduction in peak incidence and the greatest number of cases averted among the single‑lever policies. This result is consistent with an outbreak driven by R0≈2.0 and meaningful pre‑symptomatic transmission: a sharp, temporary reduction in contacts to 20 percent of baseline immediately lowers the effective reproduction number below one, pushing the peak later and lower. However, because contact rates revert to baseline after reopening, the model also shows a partial rebound unless the closure is paired with measures that persist (for example, masking or rapid testing and isolation). By contrast, the vaccination rollout administered 1,000 doses on days 70, 80, and 90 (2,000 total across the period shown) was too small and too late relative to VT’s population size to materially change epidemic trajectories. As a result, “total cases prevented” for vaccination alone was near zero or slightly negative in the accounting window, reflecting that modest, staggered coverage does not shift enough individuals from SSS to RRR early in the wave to alter transmission, while random variation and timing effects can make the comparison look unfavorable. In practical terms, these results support combining a short closure with a sustained reduction in β (for example, masking at 40 percent effectiveness from day 70 onward) and targeted testing‑isolation during and after the closure. That package maintains Rt<1 for longer, achieves a lower peak with fewer days above operational thresholds, and avoids the rebound that follows a standalone closure.

What if the vaccines were available earlier?¶

Even when the vaccine campaign delivers 2,000 doses on a single day, and even if that clinic is scheduled earlier in the wave, the effect on transmission is minimal. The core issue is scale: with a VT population of about 31,000, vaccinating 2,000 people covers roughly 6 to 7 percent of the population, which is far below what is needed to meaningfully reduce spread. With R0≈2.09, the herd‑immunity threshold is approximately 1−1/R0≈52%. A one‑off clinic at ~7 percent cannot shift enough people from SSS to RRR early enough to change the trajectory, so epidemic metrics move little and can even look worse within the finite observation window due to timing noise.

Increasing total doses to 8,000 creates a visible reduction in peak size and makes the timing of vaccination matter more, but it still does not transform outbreak dynamics on its own. Eight thousand doses correspond to about 26 percent coverage, which is still below the ~52 percent threshold implied by R0≈2.09. The model therefore shows lower peaks and some delay when doses are administered earlier, yet the epidemic persists unless vaccination is paired with measures that directly reduce contacts or shorten the infectious period. In practical terms, a rollout that starts early and continues over multiple dates performs better than a single‑day campaign, but vaccination alone at these coverage levels is not sufficient; it works best as part of a combined package with masking and rapid testing‑isolation.

Conclusions and Ethical Implications:¶

Conlcusions:

The analyses indicate a sustained early growth phase consistent with a basic reproduction number near 2.09, which aligns with steady but controllable transmission in a dense campus setting. The SEIR calibration reproduces the observed rise and approximate peak timing, and intervention experiments show that outcomes are most sensitive to early reductions in the contact rate and the effective infectious period. Among single‑lever policies, a short, well‑timed closure produces the largest downward shift in the peak, but it is prone to rebound if used alone. A combined strategy that starts on or before day 70 and couples masking at approximately 40 percent effectiveness with rapid testing and isolation maintains lower incidence for longer and avoids a sharp resurgence after reopening. Vaccination has clear value, but at the tested scales and timing windows it does not by itself move enough individuals from susceptible to recovered early in the wave to change the trajectory. The most practical conclusion is that layered measures begun early are more efficient and less disruptive than relying on any single policy.

Ethical Implications:

Public health measures are only as effective as people’s ability to comply with them, and that ability is not distributed evenly. Masking and testing reduce transmission only when students and staff can access high quality masks and reliable, no cost testing with clear paths to isolation. Closures quickly lower risk for those who can study or work remotely, but they can increase burdens for students who depend on campus jobs, on campus services, or stable housing. Vaccination policies require attention to clinic access, outreach, scheduling, and documentation so that coverage does not simply reflect existing resource gaps. Evidence from past responses shows that communities facing structural barriers, including racial and ethnic minorities, low income and uninsured individuals, non citizens, and people living in overcrowded housing, often have lower vaccination and testing access and face higher exposure in essential jobs, which can drive unequal risks and outcomes even when policies are uniformly applied. To avoid concentrating benefits among those with the most resources, implementation should include free or subsidized supplies, conveniently located and extended hours testing and vaccine clinics, targeted communication and navigation support for groups with lower access to health services, and academic or employment accommodations that reduce the cost of isolation or attending clinics. Embedding these equity safeguards into operations is not optional; broad, feasible uptake is part of the scientific requirement for an effective policy.

Limitations and Future Work:¶

Limitations.

The early growth estimate and all downstream results depend on the assumed generation time and the chosen early window. Under‑ascertainment at the start of the outbreak and subsequent behavior changes can bias growth‑rate fits if not handled carefully. The current SEIR structure captures a latent period and an infectious window but treats contact reduction and compliance as step changes and averages, which compresses real‑world heterogeneity across residence halls, classrooms, and social networks. Vaccination was modeled as discrete transfers from susceptible to recovered without explicit delays to immunity or differential uptake by subgroup. These choices are reasonable for a first pass but they limit how precisely the model can attribute effects to any single intervention.

Future work.

Three upgrades would add the most value. First, quantify uncertainty more fully by bootstrapping the early growth fit, propagating that uncertainty through to R0​, and presenting generation‑time sensitivity bands alongside every intervention result. Second, refine the transmission model with either a pre‑symptomatic infectious compartment or an explicit distribution for the infectiousness profile over time, and consider a two‑patch or networked model that allows different contact rates by setting and limited coupling between campuses. Third, evaluate operational variants that decision makers can act on, such as earlier or later start days, shorter but repeated closures, graded masking effectiveness, and vaccination schedules that include uptake limits and immunity delays. Each of these analyses should be summarized in a small decision table that reports peak size, peak day, total cases, and days above a service threshold, so the tradeoffs are visible and the recommended package can be justified clearly.
